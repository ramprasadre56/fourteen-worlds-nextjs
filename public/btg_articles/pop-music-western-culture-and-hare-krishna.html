How thousands of Soviet citizens became dedicated followers of the Krishna consciousness movement despite KGB persecution.

By Syamananda Dasa

"By Krishna's grace, they said, 'Oh, no, there is nothing dangerous. They can go.' This is the country, Moscow. I have got practical experience in going there. And always suspicious. And the people in general, they are very unhappy, very unhappy, because they have no freedom. A young man cannot go out of the country. So, they may advertise so much, but so far I have got experience: the most wretched country. Everyone is fearful." (Lecture by Srila Prabhupada on December 18, 1974 in Mumbai)

How did it happen that thousands of Soviet citizens have become dedicated followers of the Krishna consciousness movement?

The Beginning in Moscow

The story begins in the early summer of 1971. One day a young Muscovite named Anatoly Pinyayev, a lab technician at Moscow University, and his friend, the son of an Indian diplomat, encountered a strange sight near Red Square—a handsome white foreigner with the shaved head and robes of an Indian priest. Stopping to talk with him, they learned that his name was Shyamasundara and that he was accompanying his spiritual master, His Divine Grace A. C. Bhaktivedanta Swami Prabhupada, who was visiting the Soviet capital at the invitation of Professor C. G. Kotovsky, head of the department of Indian and South Asian studies at Moscow's USSR Academy of Sciences.

At Shyamasundara's invitation, Anatoly and his friend eagerly returned with him to the Hotel National for an appointment for Anatoly that would transform his life and, ultimately, the lives of thousands of Russian people. Anatoly was introduced to Srila Prabhupada and the two spoke for hours about how to practice Krishna consciousness.

Srila Prabhupada's Vision

Prabhupada saw in this young man an indication that millions of Russian people would be receptive to Krishna consciousness. He hoped that giving knowledge to this boy would spark a major spiritual revolution.

Srila Prabhupada's hopes did not go unfulfilled. Anatoly soon became his initiated disciple, receiving the name Ananta-Shanti das. Over the next several years, he travelled throughout the Soviet Union, and by his influence hundreds of Russians—including engineers, physicists, artists, and musicians—became dedicated practitioners of Krishna consciousness.

Persecution and Perseverance

But things rapidly deteriorated for the budding followers of the Hare Krishna movement in Russia. By the mid-1980s Yuri Andropov was in power, and he intensified the campaign already underway against the Hare Krishna movement. He saw devotees as representing all things religious and was determined to wipe them out. Because of Ananta-Santi's contagious enthusiasm and the staggering results of the book fairs, Semyon Tsvigun, the deputy chief of the KGB under Andropov, said that the three main threats to the Soviet Union were "pop music, Western culture, and Hare Krishna."

However later the KGB imprisoned Ananta Shanti who was then later tortured in prison and also subjected to severe mind-altering drugs to make him give up his Krishna Bhakti. But he never gave up.

A New Era

Today it is certainly heartening to see an erstwhile officer in the dreaded KGB (who is now the President of Russia) gratefully accepting a Bhagavad-gita.

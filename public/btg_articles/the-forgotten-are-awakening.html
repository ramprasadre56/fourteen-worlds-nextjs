Tribal Devotees Lead a Spiritual Renaissance from Mayapur


By Charu Bali


“TheheartofKrishnabeatsineverysoul—regardlessofcaste,color, orgeography.Butsome hearts have been waiting silently, for centuries, for someone to remind them.”


—HH Bhakti Purushottama Swami Maharaja


From the sacred grounds of Sridhama Mayapur, the global spiritual headquarters of ISKCON, a historic gathering unfolded earlier this year—one that reaffirmed Srila Prabhupada’s prophetic vision to bring Krishna consciousness to every town and village, including the often-forgotten tribal regions of India.


The Ninth Annual Tribal Care Convention, organized by the India Tribal Care Trust (ITCT), held from March 21st–23rd, was more than a mere event. It was a yajna of service, a congregation of courage, and a declaration of spiritual selfhood for over 3,000 tribal devotees who travelled from the deep forests and remote villages of Assam, Tripura, Jharkhand, Odisha, Bihar, and West Bengal—all converging at Mayapur, the lotus seat of Sri Chaitanya Mahaprabhu’s mercy.


In this article, we bring together first-hand voices, reflections from leaders, and testimonies from the ground, piecing together a living narrative of how the holy name is transforming lives in places the world seldom sees.


TheDivineVisionBehindthe Convention


Speaking to BacktoGodhead , Sri GovindaDasa, Secretary of ITCT, shared the deeper intention behind the convention: “We’re not organizing just an event—we’re facilitating a spiritual homecoming . These devotees live in hardship, but their hearts are rich in simplicity and faith. Our duty is not to change who they are, but to nurture what’s divine in them.”


He emphasized that while tribal regions often lack material development, they are fertile ground for krishna-bhakti. Where the noise of materialism hasn’t reached, the holy name can echo freely.Sri Akshayananda Dasa, a teacher from Tripura, who now leads one of ITCT’s Nimai Pathshalas, told us: “Our village once woke to the sound of conflict and alcohol. Today, the morning begins with japa, and the children chant slokas instead of shouting. We’re living Srila Prabhupada’s dream—one bead at a time.”


In Fulkushma, Bankura, the introduction of Taekwondo training at a tribal hostel has become more than just physical exercise. As a young student shared: “Learning discipline through martial arts has made me a better student of Krishna. I feel stronger—inside and out.”


Such stories are not isolated. They are growing in number as pathasalas multiply, hostels expand, and Krishna enters the lives of families once vulnerable to missionary conversions.


EmpowermentasDevotional Service


At the convention, His Excellency Sri Jishnu Dev Varma, Governor of Telangana and himself of tribal heritage, offered heartfelt words: “These are not underprivileged communities—they are keepers of dharma. Their culture is not primitive; it is pure, and through Krishna consciousness, they are reclaiming their rightful spiritual identity.”


Programs like the Tailoring Skill Initiative and the new MoU for tribal girls’ nursing education are turning empowerment into seva. As Team ITCT put it: “A sewing machine is not just a means of livelihood—it becomes a sadhana. When a mother stitches clothes while guiding her children to chant the holy names, her home transforms into a sacred temple—an abode of Lord Krishna.”


From folk dances offered as seva to devotional dramas staged by children who had never seen a stage before, the entire convention was not a performance, but an offering to Krsna. “I had tears when I saw those children chanting Sanskrit verses from Bhagavad-gita and Chaitanya-charitamrita,” shared Hridaya Chaitanya Prabhu. “This is not education—it is a return to the eternal classroom of the soul.”


For many, the highlight was witnessing elderly tribal devotees, dressed in their traditional attire, leading sankirtana with such force and sincerity that it moved even senior devotees to silence.


Beyond the Convention: Seva That Never Stops


ITCT’s work extends far beyond these three days of festivity. In July 2025, a free eye camp was organized in Bhimgarh (Birbhum, West Bengal) in collaboration with Lions Club International, providing vital care to nearly 80 villagers. Nurses were seen offering not just medicines, but also kind words and Krishna prasadam.


A volunteer Sri Tirth Harinaam Das remarked: “These weren’t just eye camps—they were windows to compassion. For many, it was the first time they felt honored, not just helped.”


Meanwhile, in Ambasa, Tripura, a new hostel was opened, providing a home where daily chanting, prasada, and Vedic values are part of the children’s upbringing. And through dry fruit nutrition drives and self-defense training, ITCT continues to demonstrate that material support and spiritual care must go hand-in-hand.


TowardaSpiritual Uprising


At the closing session, Braja Vilas Dasa proposed that the Tribal Convention be officially added to Mayapur’s annual calendar. His words echoed a new recognition: “This is not a social program—it is a spiritual uprising. “These devotees may lack material comforts, but they are wealthy in surrender.”


As the final kirtana reverberated through the convention arena, one could feel what Srila Prabhupada envisioned: a society where no one is left behind in Krishna’s plan—a movement that embraces the margins of society and places them at the center of spiritual progress.


The Tribal Care initiative is now being called “A movement within the movement.” More pathasalas are being established, more tribal leaders are being trained, and Krishna’s holy name is being carried deeper into the forests, fields, and forgotten corners of Bharat. “We are not here to convert anyone to a new faith,” said Sri Govinda Dasa. “We are here to awaken the bhakti already dormant in their hearts. This is reclamation, not replacement.” As Srila Prabhupada said, “Krishna consciousness is not an imposition on the mind. It is the original energy of the living being.”


And now, through the labor of love that is ITCT, this original energy is being kindled—in a thousand hearts, across a thousand villages.
